01

Hypothesis Testing

The Core Problem: Why We Need It

You see something in your data: a drug seems to lower blood pressure, a new button seems to lift conversions, a coin seems to land heads too often. The question that haunts every analysis is the same: is this effect real, or did I just get lucky with my sample?

You can never measure the whole population, so you take a noisy sample. Flip a fair coin 10 times and 7 heads is just randomness; flip it 1000 times and 700 heads is genuinely off. Hypothesis testing is a formal, disciplined procedure for separating real signal from random noise — it stops you from fooling yourself. Humans are catastrophically bad at this intuitively (hot hands, stocks "due" for a rebound, a redesign that "feels" better). The test forces a number onto how surprised we should be, given that nothing interesting is actually happening.

Mental Model — Proof by Contradiction, Softened In math, to prove something you assume the opposite and show it leads to absurdity. Hypothesis testing is the statistical version, softened for a noisy world. The courtroom is the cleanest analogy: the defendant is presumed innocent (the null), the prosecution must prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt (the threshold), and failing to do so yields "not guilty" — which is not "proven innocent."
CourtroomHypothesis Test
Presumed innocentNull hypothesis H₀ (nothing is happening)
The evidenceYour data
Beyond reasonable doubtSignificance threshold α
"Guilty" verdictReject H₀
"Not guilty" verdictFail to reject H₀

Null and Alternate Hypotheses

The null hypothesis (H₀) is the boring, skeptical default: "no effect," "no difference," "the coin is fair." The alternate hypothesis (H₁ or Hₐ) is the interesting claim you want to demonstrate: "the drug works," "the coin is biased."

Why is the boring statement the default? Two deep reasons:

  • The null is specific; the alternate is vague. "The coin is fair" means exactly p = 0.5 — one precise number you can build a probability model on. "The coin is biased" could be 0.51 or 0.9; you cannot compute probabilities from a vague claim. We need the precise statement to do the math, so the precise statement becomes the working assumption.
  • The burden of proof sits on the new claim. Extraordinary claims need extraordinary evidence. The data must drag you, kicking and screaming, away from disbelief.

How to Frame the Null

The null always contains the equality (=, ≤, or ≥) — it needs a specific value to assume so probabilities are computable. Whatever you set out to prove goes in H₁.

ClaimH₀H₁
New method raises avg above 70μ ≤ 70μ > 70
Coin is biased (either way)p = 0.5p ≠ 0.5
Drug changes BP (unknown direction)μ = 0μ ≠ 0

How We Test the Null — The Machinery

Step 1
Assume H₀ is true. This gives a precise probability model — a known distribution of what the data should look like in the boring world.
Step 2
Collect data, compute a test statistic: a single number measuring how far the data sits from what H₀ predicts ("how many standard errors away am I?").
Step 3
Ask the key question: if H₀ were true, how likely is data this extreme or more? That probability is the p-value.
Step 4
Compare to a pre-chosen threshold α (usually 0.05) — your "beyond reasonable doubt" bar, the risk of being fooled by noise you'll tolerate.
Step 5
Verdict: if p < α, reject H₀; if p ≥ α, fail to reject H₀.

The Language: "Reject" vs. "Fail to Reject"

The Key Asymmetry Proof by contradiction lets you do exactly one thing cleanly: show an assumption leads to absurdity and throw it out. The reverse move is invalid — if assuming H₀ does not produce absurdity, that does not prove H₀ is true. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. So we never "accept" H₀.

Two concrete reasons we can never "accept" the null:

  • Small samples hide real effects. A drug that truly lowers BP by a tiny amount, tested on 5 people, gives noisy data → you fail to reject "no effect" — but the effect is real. You simply lacked the power to see it.
  • H₀ is a single exact point. "μ = 100" or "effect is exactly zero" is almost never precisely true (maybe it's 0.0001). You can never gather enough evidence to confirm a value to infinite precision; you can only fail to find it different.
One-liner to Lock It In You can prove something is wrong; you can't prove something is right. Hypothesis testing can convict, but it can never fully exonerate. So we only ever reject or fail to reject — never accept.
Worked Intuition — no formulasFlip a coin 10 times, get 8 heads. Is it biased?
1 · Frame
H₀: p = 0.5 (fair). H₁: p ≠ 0.5 (biased).
2 · Assume H₀
If fair, heads in 10 flips follows a binomial centered at 5.
3 · Key question
How often would a fair coin give 8+ heads (or 2 or fewer, since extreme cuts both ways)? Getting 8, 9, or 10 has probability ≈ 5.5%; the symmetric low tail adds ≈ 5.5%, so "this extreme or more" ≈ 11%.
4 · Compare
p ≈ 0.11 > α = 0.05.
Fail to reject H₀. 8 heads is surprising, but not surprising enough at the 5% bar. We did not conclude the coin is fair — maybe it is slightly biased and 10 flips wasn't enough. With 1000 flips and 800 heads the story flips entirely.
Interview-Ready Summary Hypothesis testing decides whether an observed effect is real or sampling noise. Set a skeptical default (H₀) and the claim you want (H₁), assume the null, compute how likely your data is under it (the p-value), and reject if that falls below α. The logic mirrors proof by contradiction — which is why you only ever "reject" or "fail to reject," never "accept."
02

The p-value

The One Question It Answers "If the null hypothesis were true, how often would I see evidence this extreme, or more extreme, just by random luck?" The p-value is a measure of surprise — how shocked you should be by your data, assuming the boring world is the real one.
The p-value is the probability of the data (or more extreme), GIVEN the null is true — not the probability the null is true. p-value = P( data this extreme or more | H₀ is true )

The vertical bar means "given." We live inside the assumption that H₀ holds and ask how weird the data looks from in there. Small p = "if nothing interesting were happening I'd almost never see this." Large p = "even in a boring world this shows up all the time."

Why "Or More Extreme"?

For continuous data the probability of any single exact value is essentially zero, so that alone is useless. Deeper: what makes 8/10 heads surprising isn't the exact 8, it's landing far out in the tail; 9 or 10 would be even more surprising, more evidence against fairness. So "how extreme" must include everything at least as extreme — the whole tail. That tail area is the p-value.

How We Use It

  1. Set α in advance (usually 0.05). This means: "I'm willing to be fooled by noise 5% of the time."
  2. Compute the p-value.
  3. p < α → reject H₀.   p ≥ α → fail to reject H₀.

If the universe is genuinely boring and α = 0.05, you'll still wrongly reject about 1 in 20 times — the price of business (this is the Type I error rate).

Myths vs. Reality

Myth 1"The p-value is the probability that H₀ is true." No — the big one. The p-value assumes H₀ and computes the probability of the data. P(data|H₀) ≠ P(H₀|data). A p of 0.03 means "if the null were true, data this extreme occurs 3% of the time," not "3% chance the null is true." (P(dead|hanged) ≈ 1, but P(hanged|dead) is tiny — swapping the condition flips the meaning.)
Myth 2"A large p-value proves H₀ is true." It means you failed to find evidence against it — could be a real effect your sample was too small to detect.
Myth 3"The p-value tells you effect size / importance." A tiny p means you're confident the effect exists, not that it's large. With a huge sample, a trivial effect gives a microscopic p. Statistical significance ≠ practical significance. Always report effect size too.
Myth 4"p = 0.05 is a magic line." It's an arbitrary convention (Fisher). There's no physical difference between 0.049 and 0.051. The p-value is continuous evidence, not a binary switch.
Myth 5"Just collect more data / test more things until p < 0.05." That's p-hacking. Even in a boring world 1 in 20 tests crosses 0.05 by chance; test 20 things and you're guaranteed "significant" noise (the multiple-comparisons problem).

Is the p-value Only for Normal Distributions? No.

The p-value is a general concept: a tail probability under whatever distribution the null implies. Coin flips → binomial; rare counts → Poisson; variances → F; categorical → chi-squared; small-sample means → t; large-sample means → normal. The distribution changes; the logic never does.

Why Everyone Associates p-values with the Normal The Central Limit Theorem. Sample means and proportions become approximately normal for large n regardless of the population shape. Since most real testing is about means and proportions, the normal shows up constantly. It's not that p-values require normality — the CLT hands it to us for free in the common cases.

The Normal Case — the z-score

How many standard deviations away from the boring-world center did I land? Further out = more surprising = smaller p. z = observed − expected under H₀standard error

Reference points worth memorizing:

  • |z| = 1.645 → one-tailed p = 0.05
  • |z| = 1.96 → two-tailed p = 0.05  (the famous number)
  • |z| = 2.576 → two-tailed p = 0.01

One-tailed vs. two-tailed: a directional H₁ (μ > 100) looks at one tail; a non-directional H₁ (μ ≠ 100) counts both tails, so the p-value is doubled (by symmetry, 2 × one-tail area).

Two-tailed test at α = 0.05: the red tails (2.5% each) are the rejection regions. A z beyond ±1.96 lands there.

Q3 · Interpreting a p-value (conceptual)Study reports p = 0.04. Journalist writes "96% chance the treatment works." Correct?
1
p = 0.04 means P(data this extreme or more | H₀ true) = 0.04 — a statement about the data under no-effect.
2
The journalist's claim is P(H₁ true) = 0.96, i.e. P(H₀ true) = 0.04 — a statement about the hypothesis given the data.
3
The error: they flipped the conditional. P(data|H₀) was reported as P(H₀|data). Getting P(H₀|data) needs Bayes' theorem and a prior, which the p-value alone doesn't give.
4 · Correct reading
"If the treatment had no effect, results this strong or stronger would occur only 4% of the time; since that's below 5%, we reject the no-effect hypothesis."
The journalist is wrong — the most common p-value misinterpretation in popular writing.
Interview-Ready Summary The p-value is the probability of data at least as extreme as observed, assuming H₀ is true. It measures surprise, not truth. Reject when p < α. The three cardinal sins: treating it as P(H₀ true) (it's the reverse conditional), treating a large p as proof of H₀ (absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence), and confusing significance with importance (tiny effects can be highly significant with big n). It's not tied to the normal — it's a tail probability under whatever distribution H₀ implies; the normal just appears everywhere thanks to the CLT. And P(data | H₀) ≠ P(H₀ | data).

Type I & II Errors, FDR, Precision/Recall

Intuition — Two Ways to Be Wrong Reality has two states (H₀ true / false) and you make two decisions (reject / fail to reject). Two cells are correct; the other two are the two distinct failures — and they are not symmetric. Convict everyone → no innocents go free but every innocent is jailed; acquit everyone → the reverse. You cannot zero both at once.
H₀ actually TRUE (no effect)H₀ actually FALSE (real effect)
You reject H₀✗ Type I error (false positive)✓ Correct (true positive)
You fail to reject H₀✓ Correct (true negative)✗ Type II error (false negative)

Type I = False Positive (cried wolf)

Rejecting a true null — declared an effect that doesn't exist. Its probability is exactly α, by definition. Set α = 0.05 and you explicitly accept a 5% chance of crying wolf when the null is true. You set that rate yourself.

Type II = False Negative (missed the wolf)

Failing to reject a false null — a real effect existed and you missed it. Its probability is β. You don't set β directly; it depends on the true effect size, sample size, noise, and α. Detection is hard when the wolf is small (tiny effect), in fog (high variance), or glimpsed briefly (small sample).

The Trade-off

Picture two overlapping curves — the test statistic under H₀ and under H₁ — with a vertical decision line between them. Slide it right to shrink α (less false-positive tail) and you automatically grow β. The only way to shrink both is to pull the curves apart: get more data.

Which Error Is Worse? It Depends on Cost

  • Deadly-disease screening: a false negative can be fatal; a false positive is a scary follow-up. Tolerate more Type I to crush Type II → loose threshold.
  • Criminal trials: "better ten guilty go free than one innocent convicted." The false positive is deemed worse → brutally high bar, more false negatives accepted.
  • Spam filter: a real email lost to spam (FP) is worse than one spam reaching the inbox (FN) → tuned conservatively.

False Discovery Rate (FDR)

A Flipped Conditional — Not α α: of all cases where H₀ is TRUE, what fraction do I falsely flag? = P(reject | H₀ true).
FDR: of all the times I flagged something, what fraction were false alarms? = P(H₀ true | I rejected). Different number, depends on the base rate of real effects.

Why it matters — multiple comparisons. Run 1000 tests where almost nothing is real. At α = 0.05 you get ~50 false positives from noise. If only ~10 real effects exist and you catch them all, you report ~60 "discoveries," 50 of which are garbage → FDR ≈ 50/60 ≈ 83%, even though α was a respectable 0.05. When making thousands of guesses, you control the FDR (e.g. the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure), not per-test α.

Connection to Precision & Recall

Same 2×2 grid, renamed. In ML, "positive" = predicted the interesting class. FP = Type I error; FN = Type II error.

Of everything I flagged positive, what fraction was actually positive? Destroyed by false positives. Precision = TPTP + FP     and     FDR = 1 − Precision
Of all the actual positives out there, what fraction did I catch? Destroyed by false negatives. This equals statistical power, 1 − β. Recall = TPTP + FN = Power = 1 − β
The Unification The precision/recall trade-off in ML is the α/β trade-off in testing — the same line sliding between the same two overlapping distributions. Lower the threshold → more recall (fewer Type II) but less precision (more Type I). And recall is literally statistical power, which is where we go next.
Interview-Ready Summary Type I (false positive) = reject a true null, rate = α (you set it). Type II (false negative) = fail to reject a false null, rate = β. They trade off; only more data shrinks both. FDR = fraction of your rejections that are false = P(H₀|rejected), the flipped conditional of α, crucial under multiple comparisons. FP = Type I, FN = Type II; Precision = TP/(TP+FP) = 1−FDR; Recall = TP/(TP+FN) = power = 1−β.

Statistical Power & Power Analysis

Intuition If there genuinely is an effect, what's the probability my test catches it? A test with 80% power flags a true effect 80% of the time and misses it (Type II) the other 20%. Low power = a blindfolded sniper: even with a real target, you'll probably miss.
Power is your ability to detect a real effect when one exists. Power = 1 − β = P( reject H₀ | H₀ is false )

Where & Why We Use It

  • Before the study (the big one): sample-size planning. "How many samples so that, if a meaningful effect exists, I have a good chance (usually 80%) of detecting it?" Underpowered studies waste money and, in medicine, are ethically dubious — doomed to find nothing from the start.
  • After the study (diagnostic): a non-significant result from a low-power study tells you almost nothing — you can't tell "no wolf" from "eyes closed." This is why "failed to reject H₀" is such a weak conclusion.

The 80% convention (β ≤ 0.20) treats a false negative as ~4× less costly than a false positive (α = 0.05). High-stakes studies aim for 0.90–0.95.

The Four Levers

Fix any three and the fourth is determined — this is power analysis.

LeverEffect on powerIntuition
Effect size↑ bigger effect → more powerA sumo wrestler is easier to spot than a child. Usually nature's, not yours.
Sample size n↑ more data → more powerCurves get skinnier and pull apart. Your main knob.
α↑ looser α → more powerEasier to reject — but more false positives. Buying power with α.
Variability σ↓ more noise → less powerFatter, more overlapping curves. Reduce via better measurement / paired designs.
Power is the area of the H₁ curve falling in the "reject" region. Pull the curves apart (bigger effect, more data, less noise) or move the threshold → more power.

Interview-Ready Summary Power = 1 − β = probability of detecting a real effect. It depends on effect size, sample size, α, and variability. Power analysis solves for sample size (usually targeting 80%); because effect size enters as d², detecting an effect half as large needs ~4× the data.

t-Distribution, Degrees of Freedom & the t-Test

The Problem That Created the t-Distribution

Everything with z-scores assumed we knew the population σ. But in reality you almost never do — if you knew σ that precisely you'd probably know μ too. So you estimate σ from the sample with the sample SD, s. The catch: s is itself a noisy estimate from the same small sample. You've injected a second source of uncertainty. Naively using the normal with a noisy s makes you overconfident — you'd reject too often because the normal's tails are too thin to account for it.

The Solution — Fatter Tails William Gosset ("Student," because Guinness forbade publishing) showed that replacing σ with s yields a fatter-tailed distribution: the t-distribution. Because s can underestimate σ (small denominator → big statistic), extreme values are more common, so the t-distribution assigns them more probability. This makes the test appropriately more conservative — "since you're guessing the spread, I'll demand stronger evidence."

Degrees of Freedom

The t-distribution is a family of curves indexed by degrees of freedom (df). More df → thinner tails → closer to the normal; by df ≈ 30 they're nearly identical; at df = ∞ they're the same. More data → s is a more reliable stand-in for σ → less extra uncertainty → convergence to normal.

What df Actually Is The number of values free to vary once constraints are imposed. Tell me three numbers must average 10 (sum to 30): you freely pick the first two, but the third is forced. Three numbers, one constraint → 2 df. Computing s needs the sample mean first; once it's fixed, deviations from it must sum to zero — one constraint — leaving n − 1 df. That's why sample variance divides by n−1 (Bessel's correction).
The t-distribution has fatter tails and a shorter peak than the normal. As df grows it converges onto the normal curve.

Finding df by Test

Testdf
One-sample t-testn − 1 (one mean estimated)

General principle: df = (independent observations) − (parameters estimated from the data). Every parameter you estimate costs one df.

What the t-Test Is & Why It's "Better/Worse" Than z

Just like z, but with the sample's estimated spread s in place of the known σ. t = x̄ − μ₀s / √n
  • One-sample: is my mean different from a claimed value?
  • Two-sample (independent): do two groups' means differ? (A/B testing bread and butter)
  • Paired: same subjects measured twice (before/after).

Better (more honest): the t-test accounts for the uncertainty in estimating σ. Since you essentially never know σ, it's the honest choice; a z-test with estimated σ on a small sample gives artificially small p-values and inflated false positives. "Worse" (only narrowly): it's more conservative — fat tails push critical values out (e.g. ~2.78 at df=4 vs 1.96 for z), so slightly less power. But that "extra power" of z was an illusion from pretending you had certainty you didn't. As n grows the two become identical, so the cost vanishes. The t-test is correctly humble — the safer, more defensible choice.

Reject H₀ (|t| = 2.811 > 2.262, p ≈ 0.020). If the true mean were 350, a sample this far off with this variability happens only ~2% of the time. Aside: a z-test here would have given p ≈ 0.005 — overconfident. The t-test reins that in because with 10 cups, s = 9 is genuinely uncertain.
Interview-Ready Summary Estimating σ from the sample adds uncertainty, giving a fatter-tailed t-distribution indexed by df (independent obs minus parameters estimated; n−1 for one sample). It converges to the normal as df grows. Use z if σ is known (or n large); use t if σ is estimated (mandatory for small n, always defensible). The t-test uses s in place of σ; it's the honest, more conservative choice, and its only "cost" — reduced power — was illusory confidence z never legitimately had.

The z-Test & the z-vs-t Comparison

A z-test is a hypothesis test whose statistic follows the standard normal under the null — the light-bulb and conversion-rate problems were z-tests. Its defining feature: you either know the population σ, or n is large enough that estimating it is negligible. It follows a normal shape because of the CLT, and all its machinery (1.96, tail-area p-values) rests on that.

Chi-Squared Distribution & Tests

Where the Chi-Squared Distribution Comes From

Build it from the normal. Take a standard normal Z, square it, and sum several independent squared standard normals:

A chi-squared distribution is what you get when you sum k squared standard normals. The parameter k is just how many you added. χ²k = Z₁² + Z₂² + … + Zk²

This immediately gives its properties, each with intuition:

  • Never negative — you're summing squares. Lives on [0, ∞).
  • Right-skewed — most squared normals are small (a normal is usually near 0), but occasionally a large value squares to something huge, stretching a long right tail. Skew is strongest for small k.
  • Center grows with k — mean = k, because each squared standard normal contributes E[Z²] = 1 on average.
  • Becomes more symmetric (normal-looking) as k grows — the CLT again: summing many independent things trends toward normal.
Higher df shifts the chi-squared right and makes it more symmetric. Mean equals the df.
Why It's Useful Whenever you measure how far observed things deviate from expected and want all deviations to count positively regardless of direction, you square them ("off by −3" and "off by +3" both become 9), then sum for a total "how wrong was everything" score. That total naturally follows a chi-squared. So it's the home of any test based on summed squared deviations from expectation.

Test 1 — Goodness-of-Fit

Question: does observed categorical data match a hypothesized distribution? (A die rolled 120 times: if fair, expect 20 each.)

For each category: how far is observed from expected, squared (direction-free, big misses punished), divided by expected (scale the deviation), then summed. χ² = ∑ (Oi − Ei)²Ei

Why divide by Ei? A deviation of 10 means something different when you expected 20 vs 2000. Dividing normalizes "how surprising is this miss relative to scale," which is exactly what turns each term into an approximately squared-standard-normal — so the sum is chi-squared. df = categories − 1 (the counts must sum to n). A large χ² means big deviation → evidence against H₀; chi-squared tests are always right-tailed.

Test 2 — Test of Independence

Question: are two categorical variables related, or independent? Build a contingency table; if independent, expected cell counts come from the margins (the multiplication rule P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B)).

If independent, a cell's expected count is its row total times column total over the grand total. Eij = (rowi total) × (colj total)grand total
χ² = ∑cells (Oij − Eij)²Eij     df = (rows − 1)(cols − 1)

Parameters & Practical Caveat

The chi-squared distribution has exactly one parameter: df, which sets its whole shape. You compute df from the problem's structure (categories minus constraints), then use it to get the p-value. Inputs to any test: observed counts O, expected counts E (under H₀), and df. Caveat: the approximation breaks when expected counts are tiny (rule of thumb: every E ≥ 5); for tiny counts use an exact test (Fisher's exact for 2×2).

1 · Frame
H₀: fair (each face 1/6). H₁: not fair.

Interview-Ready Summary Chi-squared = sum of k squared standard normals; non-negative, right-skewed, centered at its single parameter df, → normal as df grows. Natural distribution for summed squared deviations from expectation. Tests (goodness-of-fit, independence) use ∑(O−E)²/E; df is (categories−1) or (rows−1)(cols−1); always right-tailed; needs expected counts ≥ 5.

The F-Distribution

Chi-squared describes variance-like things (summed squared deviations). To compare two variances we take their ratio:

Take two independent chi-squared variables, each divided by its own df, and form their ratio — that ratio is the F-distribution. It's a ratio of two scaled variances. F = χ²₁ / d₁χ²₂ / d₂
  • Never negative (ratio of non-negatives); lives on [0, ∞).
  • Right-skewed (inherited from chi-squared).
  • Centers near 1 — if the two variances are equal, the ratio is ~1. F >> 1 means the numerator's variation dwarfs the denominator's: the signal we hunt.
  • Two df parameters (numerator d₁, denominator d₂) — it's built from two chi-squared pieces, so F-tables are indexed by two numbers.

When we use it: directly comparing two variances (an F-test for equality of variances — "do these two processes have the same consistency?"), and — the dominant use — ANOVA, which reframes a question about means into a ratio of variances.

09

Tests for Variances & ANOVA

The Problem ANOVA Solves

You have three or more groups and want to know if their means differ. First instinct: run a t-test on every pair. With 3 groups that's 3 tests; it explodes from there.

Why Many Pairwise t-Tests Fail Every t-test carries 5% false-positive risk. Run many and the risks accumulate. With 3 groups, P(at least one false positive) ≈ 1 − (0.95)³ ≈ 14%, climbing fast with more groups. You'd nearly guarantee "finding" a difference that isn't real — the multiple-comparisons / family-wise error problem.

ANOVA's solution: one single test asking "are any of these means different?" while holding the overall false-positive rate at α.

What ANOVA Tests

  • H₀: all group means equal (μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ = …)
  • H₁: at least one differs (it does not say which)
The Beautiful Core Idea — Test Means by Comparing Two Variances Between-group variance: how far the group means sit from the grand mean — variation caused by groups differing (the signal). Within-group variance: scatter inside each group around its own mean — natural noise unrelated to groups.

If H₀ is true (all means equal), group means differ only by the same noise that drives within-group scatter, so the two variances are roughly equal → ratio ≈ 1. If means genuinely differ, between-group variance balloons beyond noise → ratio >> 1. The spread of the group means is the fingerprint of whether the means differ.
F is the ratio of variation explained by group differences to leftover within-group noise — signal over noise. F = between-group variancewithin-group variance = signal (do group means spread apart?)noise (within-group scatter)
  • F ≈ 1: between-group is no bigger than noise → means look the same → fail to reject.
  • F >> 1: means spread further than chance allows → reject; at least one differs.

Because it's a ratio of two variances, it follows the F-distribution, with df₁ = (groups − 1) and df₂ = (total observations − groups).

If the group lines spread far from the grand mean relative to the within-group dot-scatter, F is large → the means differ.

Needed / When to Use / Follow-up

Needed because many pairwise t-tests inflate false positives; ANOVA gives one global test at controlled α. Use when one categorical factor has 3+ levels and a continuous outcome. (With exactly 2 groups, ANOVA reduces to the t-test: F = t².) Follow-up: ANOVA says only that some difference exists, not which; if you reject, run post-hoc pairwise tests corrected for multiple comparisons (e.g. Tukey's HSD). Assumptions: roughly equal variances and normal residuals; otherwise use Welch's ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis.

Interview-Ready Summary The F-distribution is a ratio of two scaled chi-squared variables (a ratio of variances): non-negative, right-skewed, centered near 1, two df parameters. ANOVA is "analysis of variance" but a test about means, comparing 3+ groups in one test that controls the false-positive rate (unlike many t-tests, which inflate it). It compares between-group variance (signal) to within-group variance (noise) as an F-ratio: F ≈ 1 = no difference, F >> 1 = at least one mean differs. It tells you that, not which — follow with corrected post-hoc tests.

Order Statistics

The concept: sort your data smallest to largest; each value in its sorted position is an order statistic. The smallest is the 1st, the largest the nth, the middle one the median. They're your data values ranked by position rather than by collection order.

Intuition Once sorted, positions carry information independent of the raw values. Min, max, median, quartiles, percentiles are all order statistics. The power: ranks behave predictably even when raw values are messy, skewed, or on a weird scale.

Where we use it: medians and percentiles (the basis of box plots), the foundation of nearly all non-parametric tests (which work on ranks), extreme-value analysis (floods, insurance tails, p99 latency — literally an order statistic), and robust statistics that resist outliers. The connecting idea: replace raw values with their ranks and you no longer depend on normality — the door into non-parametric testing.

11

Wilcoxon & Kolmogorov-Smirnov Tests

Wilcoxon Test

What it is: a non-parametric alternative to the t-test. Two versions: the signed-rank test (counterpart to the paired t-test) and the rank-sum test / Mann-Whitney U (counterpart to the two-sample t-test).

Intuition Throw away raw values, work only with their ranks (order statistics in action). If two groups are truly the same, their values interleave when pooled and ranked, so ranks are shared roughly evenly. If one group is systematically larger, its values cluster at the high ranks. The test detects that rank imbalance.

Where we use it: when the t-test's assumptions break — small samples, non-normal data, ordinal data (1–5 ratings), outliers that would wreck a mean. Because it uses ranks, a wild outlier just becomes "the highest rank" rather than dragging the result. Trade-off: slightly less powerful than the t-test when the data really is normal — you give up a little sensitivity for not needing normality.

Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) Goodness-of-Fit

What it is: a test for whether a sample came from a particular distribution (one-sample), or whether two samples share a distribution (two-sample).

Intuition Build the cumulative distribution of your data (the running "fraction of data at or below this value" as you sweep low to high). Compare that staircase to the reference's cumulative curve (or to another sample's). The test takes the single biggest vertical gap between the two curves. Small max gap = good fit; large gap = they diverge somewhere.
The KS statistic is the largest vertical distance between the two cumulative curves. Big gap → different distributions.

Where we use it: checking whether data is plausibly normal (or any distribution) before a test that assumes it; comparing two samples for the same distribution — in ML this is a classic way to detect data drift (feature distribution in training vs. production). It's fully general: no assumption about the shape, just the biggest gap between cumulative curves.

The Thread Order statistics (ranks, cumulative positions) are the raw material. Wilcoxon ranks values and checks if two groups share ranks fairly (a test about location/central tendency); KS builds cumulative curves and checks the biggest gap (a test about the whole distribution's shape). One asks "is one group bigger?", the other "are these the same distribution at all?"

Bayesian Estimation & MAP

The Core Shift Frequentist (MLE) world: a parameter is a fixed unknown constant; the data is random. Bayesian world: the parameter itself is a random variable with a distribution of belief. You don't think the conversion rate is one mystery number; you hold a distribution — "probably ~12%, plausibly 8–16%." The payoff: a principled way to inject prior knowledge and to express how uncertain your estimate is.

Prior, Likelihood, Posterior

  • Prior P(θ): belief about the parameter before data. Where outside knowledge enters.
  • Likelihood P(data|θ): how probable the observed data is for each θ — the same likelihood as MLE.
  • Posterior P(θ|data): updated belief after data — the output.
Your updated belief is your prior reweighted by how well each parameter value explains the data you actually saw. P(θ | data) ∝ P(data | θ) × P(θ)
posterior ∝ likelihood × prior  (the denominator P(data) is just a normalizing constant)
Mental Model Start with a belief (prior), the data pulls and reshapes it (likelihood), you end with the posterior — a blend of "what I thought" and "what the data says," weighted by how strong each one is.

Why & When We Use It

  • Meaningful prior knowledge (a hundred similar A/B tests landing near 10–15%). MLE throws that away; Bayesian updates from it.
  • Scarce data. 3 heads in 3 flips: MLE says P(heads) = 1.0 ("never tails"). A reasonable prior pulls it back to sanity. Less data → prior protects more; as data grows the prior fades.
  • Need uncertainty, not just a point. The posterior is a full distribution → "95% probability the rate is 9–14%" (a credible interval).
  • Continuous updating. Today's posterior is tomorrow's prior — powers bandits, spam filters, recommenders.

MAP — Maximum A Posteriori

The full posterior is rich, but often you want one best number. MAP asks: what single θ is most probable given data and prior? — the peak (mode) of the posterior.

Pick the parameter value that maximizes the posterior — the most likely value after combining prior with data. θ̂MAP = arg maxθ P(θ | data) = arg maxθ P(data | θ) P(θ)
MLE for comparison — identical except MAP includes the prior term P(θ). θ̂MLE = arg maxθ P(data | θ)
Complementary or Replacement? — MAP Generalizes MLE MAP with a flat (uniform) prior IS MLE. A uniform P(θ) is constant, and multiplying the likelihood by a constant doesn't move the maximum — so MAP collapses into MLE. MLE is the special case of MAP claiming no prior preference. Use MLE with no meaningful prior or abundant data; use MAP with prior knowledge or scarce data needing regularization. As data grows, MAP converges to MLE (the likelihood swamps any fixed prior). They diverge most when data is scarce — exactly when the prior is most valuable.

Why MAP Is Everywhere in ML

Regularization IS MAP Estimation Not an analogy — mathematically exact. Pure MLE minimizes raw loss and overfits. Adding a regularization penalty on the weights is literally a prior on the parameters, and minimizing (loss + penalty) is literally MAP.
  • L2 (Ridge / weight decay) = MAP with a Gaussian prior on the weights ("weights should be small, near zero"). The prior's belief shrinks them.
  • L1 (Lasso) = MAP with a Laplace prior. The Laplace's sharp spike at zero is why L1 drives weights to exactly zero — sparsity / feature selection.

The regularization strength λ is the strength of your prior: strong λ = confident prior = data must fight harder; weak λ = vague prior = data dominates. Regularization is everywhere in ML, and regularization is MAP — that's why MAP is everywhere.

Q1 · MAP vs MLE — the scarce-data rescue3 flips, 3 heads. (a) MLE estimate? (b) MAP with a "probably fair" prior?
(a) MLE
Likelihood θ³ is maximized by pushing θ as high as possible:
θ̂MLE = headstotal = 33 = 1.0
Absurd — "never tails" from 3 flips. The classic small-data failure.
(b) MAP
Add a Beta prior acting like pseudo-counts — say 2 prior heads + 2 prior tails (gentle "probably fair"):
θ̂MAP = real heads + prior headsreal total + prior total = 3 + 23 + 4 = 57 ≈ 0.71
MAP ≈ 0.71 — data pulled belief up from 0.5, but the prior refuses the insane "tails impossible." It acted as a sanity-preserving regularizer. Convergence: with 1000 heads in 1000 flips, MAP = (1000+2)/(1000+4) ≈ 0.998 — the 4 pseudo-counts are now negligible. With lots of data, MAP ≈ MLE.
Q2 · MAP of a mean with a Gaussian priorServers historically ~100 ms (prior); a few measurements give sample mean 130 ms. Where does MAP land?
1 · Prior
Centered at 100 ms, spread = how sure you are (prior variance).
2 · Likelihood
Centered at the sample mean 130 ms, spread = noise & sample size (more samples → tighter).
3 · Posterior / MAP
For Gaussian prior & likelihood, MAP is a precision-weighted average (precision = 1/variance = confidence):
Lands between 100 and 130, pulled toward whichever side is more confident (smaller variance). θ̂MAP = (precprior × 100) + (precdata × 130)precprior + precdata

Few/noisy measurements → weak likelihood → MAP stays near 100.

The whole Bayesian story in one formula: a tug-of-war between belief and data, the rope pulled by confidence on each side. The vague-prior limit recovering MLE (≈130) is "MAP with a flat prior = MLE" shown numerically.
Interview-Ready Summary Bayesian estimation treats the parameter as a random variable with a distribution of belief. Start with a prior, multiply by the likelihood, get a posterior (updated belief). Used with prior knowledge, scarce data (the prior regularizes and prevents absurd estimates), and when you need full uncertainty. MAP is the posterior's mode — identical to MLE except it multiplies by the prior. MAP generalizes MLE: flat prior → MAP = MLE, and MAP → MLE as data grows. In ML, regularization is MAP: L2 = Gaussian prior, L1/Lasso = Laplace prior, and λ is the prior's strength. MLE when no useful prior or abundant data; MAP to encode prior knowledge or tame scarce, noisy data.

Sample Size vs. Effective Sample Size

Sample size (n): how many data points you collected. Effective sample size (ESS): how many independent, equally-informative points you effectively have — often far fewer. Not every point carries a full unit of fresh information.

Why They Diverge

  • Correlation between observations. Measuring one patient's BP 100 times in an hour isn't 100 independent facts — readings are highly correlated. Time-series, repeated measures, clustered data (students within a classroom) all suffer this. More correlation → lower ESS.
  • Unequal weighting. If a few responses are heavily weighted (one stands in for 50 people), a handful dominate and the effective sample shrinks.
  • Importance sampling / MCMC. Samples drawn from the wrong distribution and reweighted; ESS measures how many "good" samples you really have. Common formula ESS = (∑wi)² / ∑wi² — equals n only when all weights are equal, collapses toward 1 when one weight dominates.
Why It Matters Every formula with √n (standard error, power, CI width) assumes independence. If points are correlated, plugging in raw n makes you overconfident — intervals too narrow, p-values too small. Use ESS. Classic A/B trap: the same user appears many times; counting events instead of users inflates n far above ESS and manufactures false significance.
14

Standard Deviation vs. Standard Error

Standard deviation (SD) describes the spread of individual data points — "how much do observations vary around the mean?" It's the square root of variance, a property of the data. Standard error (SE) describes the spread of a statistic (usually the mean) across hypothetical repeated samples — "if I redid the experiment many times, how much would my sample mean bounce around?" A property of your estimate, not the data.

The standard error is the standard deviation shrunk by the square root of how many points you averaged over. SE = SD√n
Why SE Is Smaller & Shrinks with n A single random person can be very tall or short (high SD). But the average of 100 people is far more stable — highs and lows cancel. More averaging → more cancellation → more stable mean → smaller SE. This is the CLT and LLN made concrete.
The One-Line Distinction SD tells you how spread out the data is; SE tells you how precisely you've pinned down the mean. SD does not shrink with more data (it converges to the true population spread — a fixed fact of the world); SE does shrink (the mean gets pinned down more precisely). Describe variability of individuals → report SD. Express confidence in the mean → report SE (CIs are built from it: mean ± 1.96 × SE). Common sin: showing tiny SE error bars where SD belongs, making variability look smaller than it is.

Bootstrapping

Core idea: estimate the uncertainty of any statistic by resampling your own data, with replacement, many times. No formula, no distributional assumption — let the data simulate "what other samples might have looked like."

Why It's Almost Magical You wish you could re-run the experiment thousands of times to see how your statistic varies — but you only have one sample. The insight: treat your sample as a stand-in for the population and draw new samples from it. Your sample is your best picture of the population, so resampling from it mimics resampling from the population.

Mechanics

  1. You have a sample of size n.
  2. Draw n points from it with replacement (some appear multiple times, others not at all) — one "bootstrap sample."
  3. Compute your statistic (mean, median, correlation, 90th percentile…) on it.
  4. Repeat thousands of times → thousands of values of the statistic.
  5. Their spread estimates the standard error; their percentiles give a CI (2.5th & 97.5th → 95% CI).

Why "with replacement"? Without it you'd just get your original dataset back every time — no variation. Replacement creates the variation that mimics genuine resampling. Why valuable: works for statistics with no clean SE formula (median, percentiles, ratios, correlations, model metrics) and makes no distributional assumption.

Using Bootstrapping to Calculate a p-value

Simulate the null by resampling. Example — do two groups have different means?

1 · State H₀
Both groups come from the same distribution. Compute the observed statistic (actual difference in means).
2 · Simulate the null
Under H₀ the labels are meaningless, so pool all data and randomly reshuffle which points are "A" vs "B" (a permutation test, bootstrapping's sibling). Recompute the difference each time.
3 · Build the null distribution
Repeat thousands of times → thousands of differences arising purely by chance when there's no real effect.
4 · Count for the p-value
Out of all the fake differences generated assuming no effect, what fraction were as big as the real one? p = # resampled statistics ≥ observedtotal resamples
Why Beautiful A p-value with zero formulas, no z/t/χ², no distributional assumptions — you simulated the null directly by shuffling and counted how often chance alone is as extreme as observed. That's the literal meaning of a p-value, made tangible. When data is weird or assumptions are shaky, this is the most honest route.

Population vs. Sample Variance — Why n−1

Population variance: you know the true mean μ (you have the whole population). σ² = 1N ∑ (xi − μ)²
Sample variance: you must use the sample mean x̄ because you don't know μ — and you divide by n−1, not n. s² = 1n − 1 ∑ (xi − x̄)²
The Intuition (Understand First) You don't know μ, so you substitute x̄ — computed from the same data. Key fact: x̄ is, by construction, the value closest to your data points. The sum ∑(xi − c)² is minimized at c = x̄; any other center (including the true μ) gives a larger sum. So measuring spread around x̄ uses the point that hugs your data most tightly — making the squared deviations systematically too small. You underestimate the true spread. The fix: divide by a smaller number (n−1) to inflate the estimate by exactly the right amount.
Degrees-of-Freedom View Computing x̄ imposes one constraint — deviations from x̄ must sum to zero. Know n−1 of them and the last is forced. You have only n−1 independent pieces of information about the spread; one df was spent on the mean. Divide by the number of independent deviations.

The Proof (Why Exactly n−1)

We want an unbiased estimator — its average over all possible samples equals the true σ². Start from the identity linking deviations-from-x̄ to deviations-from-μ:

∑ (xi − x̄)² = ∑ (xi − μ)² − n(x̄ − μ)²

In words: scatter around the sample mean equals scatter around the true mean minus a positive term n(x̄−μ)² — exactly the "shrinkage" from using x̄ instead of μ. (It comes from expanding (xi−μ)² = ((xi−x̄)+(x̄−μ))² and summing; the cross term vanishes because ∑(xi−x̄) = 0.) Take expectations of both sides:

First term: each point's expected squared deviation from the true mean is σ², and there are n of them. E[ ∑(xi − μ)² ] = nσ²
Second term: uses Var(x̄) = σ²/n — the squared standard error. E[ n(x̄ − μ)² ] = n · Var(x̄) = n · σ²n = σ²
Putting it together — there it is: E[ ∑(xi − x̄)² ] = nσ² − σ² = (n − 1)σ²

The expected sum of squared deviations around x̄ is exactly (n−1)σ², not nσ². Therefore:

  • Divide by n: E[s²] = (n−1)σ²/n < σ² — biased downward, underestimates (confirming the intuition).
  • Divide by n−1: E[s²] = (n−1)σ²/(n−1) = σ² — unbiased.
The Punchline n−1 isn't a fudge — it's the unique factor making the expectation land exactly on the true variance. The "missing" σ² (subtracted because we used x̄ instead of μ) is precisely one data point's worth of information — hence n−1. The bias is large for small n (÷4 vs ÷5 = 25% at n=5) and negligible for large n.

Odds & Log-Odds

Odds: the ratio of "happens" to "doesn't happen." p = 0.75 → odds 3 ("3 to 1"). p = 0.5 → odds 1. odds = p1 − p
Log-odds (the logit): the natural log of the odds. logit(p) = ln( p1 − p )

The Ranges — The Whole Reason Log-Odds Dominates

Probability is trapped in [0,1]; odds in [0,∞) but lopsided; log-odds spans the full real line, symmetric around 0.

Why Take the Log — What It Solves

  • Unbounded range solves the modeling problem. Linear models output w·x + b ∈ (−∞,∞), but probability is trapped in [0,1] — a mismatch. Log-odds also spans all reals, so you cleanly set log-odds = w·x + b. This is exactly logistic regression: model log-odds linearly, then convert back to probability via the sigmoid (the sigmoid is the inverse of the logit). The log-odds is the bridge that lets a linear model produce a valid probability.
  • Symmetry around zero is interpretable. 0 → p=0.5; positive = more likely than not, negative = less, magnitude = strength. ln(2) ≈ +0.69 and ln(0.5) ≈ −0.69 are exact mirrors — "twice as likely" and "half as likely" become symmetric.
  • Addition replaces multiplication (the deep reason). Logs turn multiplication into addition. Odds combine multiplicatively (awkward); in log-odds space, evidence adds up. Each feature contributes additively — so logistic-regression coefficients are interpretable ("this feature adds 0.7 to the log-odds"), and it connects to information theory / log-likelihoods / "bits of evidence" (Naive Bayes sums log-probabilities for the same reason).
  • Numerical stability (engineering reason). Probabilities can be tiny (10⁻³⁰⁰); multiplying many underflows to zero. Log space turns products into sums of manageable numbers. ML libraries compute log-probabilities / log-odds internally — cross-entropy, log-loss, softmax-with-logits. The "logits" a network outputs are unnormalized log-odds; sigmoid/softmax converts back only at the very end.
Interview-Ready Summary Log-odds maps the bounded, asymmetric probability scale onto the full, symmetric real line. That makes it the natural output space for linear models (logistic regression sets log-odds = w·x + b), turns multiplicative evidence-combination into additive contributions (interpretable coefficients), and gives numerical stability by replacing tiny-probability multiplication with addition. That trio — unbounded range, additivity, stability — is why log-odds is everywhere in ML.

CLT & LLN in Inferential Statistics

Law of Large Numbers — Why Estimation Works At All

LLN: as the sample grows, the sample mean converges to the true population mean. It's the reason estimation is even possible — the guarantee that with enough data your estimates home in on truth rather than wandering off. It underwrites:

  • Consistency of estimators — MLE, sample mean, etc. converge to the right answer with enough data.
  • The shrinking standard error — SE = SD/√n → 0 as n grows is LLN in action.
  • Bootstrapping's validity — a large sample faithfully represents the population, so resampling from it approximates resampling from the population.

But LLN alone isn't enough — it says the mean converges, not how it's distributed or how uncertain it is along the way. That's CLT's job.

Central Limit Theorem — Why We Can Compute p-values & CIs

CLT: regardless of the population's shape, the distribution of the sample mean becomes approximately normal as n grows, centered at the true mean with SD = σ/√n. The single most important theorem in inference, because:

  • It's why z/t-tests work on non-normal data. Raw data can be wildly skewed, but the sample mean is approximately normal for decent n. Since tests about means use the distribution of the mean, they're valid even when the data isn't normal — you can t-test skewed income data. (This is the "why p-values associate with the normal" answer, fully grounded.)
  • It's why CIs have their form. "mean ± 1.96 × SE" comes directly from CLT: because the mean is normal, 95% of the time it lands within 1.96 SE of the truth. No CLT, no 1.96.
  • It's why the SE formula is meaningful — CLT pins the mean's spread as exactly σ/√n, giving the scale for all tests and intervals.
  • It's the root of the χ², t, F family. Each was built from normal variables; the reason those normals appear in real data is CLT. So CLT is the root of the entire distribution family tree.
The Division of Labor LLN promises your estimate converges to the truth (so estimation is possible); CLT tells you the shape and spread of that estimate's uncertainty (so you can quantify it with tests and intervals). LLN says "you'll get there"; CLT says "and here's exactly how uncertain you are on the way." Together they make the leap from a finite, noisy sample to a confident statement about an unseen population logically valid — everything else in this series is built on these two guarantees.
